# Business Model Canvas

## What it is in Aim Praxis

The Business Model Canvas (BMC), originally developed by Alexander Osterwalder, is treated in Aim Praxis as the outermost layer of the seven-layer system. It operates at the cycle time of weeks to quarters and is responsible for the system aim, customer segments, value flows, revenue model, and cost structure.

The standard BMC has nine boxes: customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, and cost structure. Aim Praxis reads these nine boxes as a stock-and-flow system rather than a static worksheet.

## The systems reading

Each of the nine boxes is a stock with inflows and outflows. The boxes are connected by three flows that form a cycle:

**Value creation flow.** Key partnerships and key resources combine through key activities to produce the value proposition. This is the production side of the business.

**Value delivery flow.** The value proposition reaches customer segments through channels, mediated by customer relationships. This is the distribution side.

**Value capture flow.** Customer segments generate revenue streams, which fund cost structure, which sustains the key resources and activities needed for continued value creation. This is the loop that makes the system continuous rather than one-shot.

A business model works when the cycle is closed and the rates are sustainable. It fails when value is created but not delivered, delivered but not captured, or captured at rates that don't fund continued creation.

## Three feedback loops

The BMC implies three feedback loops:

**Growth loop (reinforcing).** Revenue funds key activities, which improve the value proposition, which attracts more customers, which generates more revenue. Healthy when closed; produces growth. Broken when revenue does not fund the right activities or when activities do not improve the proposition.

**Efficiency loop (balancing).** Costs drain capital, which constrains key resources, which limits key activities, which sets cost structure. Pushes the business toward equilibrium between production and consumption. Pathological when consumption exceeds production.

**Learning loop (reinforcing when healthy).** Customer interactions reveal whether the value proposition matches actual needs. Information flows back to refine key activities. Most business model failures are failures of this loop — the business is operationally healthy but cognitively isolated from its market.

## Hidden stocks

The standard BMC omits several stocks that significantly affect business behavior:

**Brand and reputation.** Slow-moving stock that modulates acquisition rates, channel effectiveness, and partnership availability.

**Operational knowledge.** Accumulated learning about how the business actually works. Distinguishes a ten-year-old business from a startup with the same canvas.

**Customer goodwill.** Distinct from customer count. Affects referral, expansion revenue, and churn resistance.

**Cash reserves.** Buffer between revenue and costs. Determines resilience to disruption.

Aim Praxis explicitly names these as part of the BMC analysis even though they are not in Osterwalder's original canvas.

## What the BMC catches

The BMC catches direction error — the product is heading the wrong way. No inner-loop discipline can catch this. A team can deploy continuously with perfect test coverage and still build software for a market that doesn't exist.

This is the layer's irreducible function. Eliminating it makes the entire system blind to direction error.

## Cadence

Recommended cadence: quarterly review with annual strategic overhaul. The BMC is not a static document filled in at company founding. It is the team's current articulation of what the business is, updated as the team's understanding evolves.

The quarterly review asks: are the nine boxes still accurate? Have the rates changed? Are the loops still functioning? Is the aim still aligned with what the team actually believes?

## Integration with VPC

The BMC's value proposition and customer segments boxes are the entry points for the VPC layer. The VPC zooms into that specific connection and decomposes it into the supplier-customer interface analysis. The BMC defines what the proposition is and who it serves; the VPC analyzes whether the proposition actually fits the segment.
