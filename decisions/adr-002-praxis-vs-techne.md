# ADR-002: Praxis vs Techne

**Status:** Accepted

**Date:** 2026-05-19

## Context

In choosing the methodology's name, the central word "praxis" was selected over alternatives. The most significant alternative considered was the Aristotelian "techne" — the parallel concept in Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* that names productive craft rather than disciplined practice.

The distinction matters because the choice between praxis and techne signals what the methodology fundamentally is — and the methodology's identity depends on getting this right.

## Decision

The methodology is named with **praxis**, not techne. The methodology is characterized as disciplined practice informed by theory, with the formation of the practitioner as part of its purpose.

## The Aristotelian distinction

Aristotle distinguishes several modes of human activity:

- **Theoria** — contemplative knowing, the activity of pure intellectual understanding
- **Praxis** — action whose end is in the doing itself, the activity of living well
- **Poiesis** — making, production of something separate from the maker
- **Techne** — productive craft, the skilled knowledge that produces good poiesis

In Aristotle's strict scheme, software development is closer to techne (skilled production of an artifact, the software) than to praxis (action whose end is in the doing).

This is why "techne" was seriously considered. Software development *does* produce artifacts; the artifacts *are* the point in some sense.

## Why praxis nonetheless

Several reasons justify praxis over techne despite the apparent Aristotelian fit of techne:

**The modern usage of praxis has broadened.** In contemporary usage (especially after Hegel, Marx, and the philosophical tradition that followed), "praxis" has come to mean the integration of theory and practice generally — disciplined practice informed by understanding. This broader sense fits the methodology better than Aristotle's strict sense fits techne.

**The methodology forms its practitioners.** Aim Praxis is not just a way to produce software; it is a way that, over time, changes how its practitioners think. The compounding artifacts compound not just in the project but in the practitioners. This formative aspect is praxis-like; techne is more strictly about the produced artifact.

**The methodology has theoretical depth.** Techne in Aristotle's sense is skilled know-how; praxis is action informed by understanding. Aim Praxis explicitly integrates theoretical understanding (Deming, systems thinking) with practical action. The integration is the praxis aspect.

**The aesthetic of "praxis" matches.** The word has weight, signals seriousness, and feels appropriate for the methodology's substance. "Techne" feels more clinical, more focused on the artifact.

**The philosophical lineage is praxis-friendly.** The methodology draws on Dallas Willard and the broader tradition of treating disciplined practice as both intellectually serious and spiritually formative. This tradition is praxis-aligned.

## What this means for the methodology

The naming choice has consequences for how the methodology presents itself:

- The methodology emphasizes practitioner formation, not just artifact production
- The methodology integrates theoretical understanding with practical action
- The methodology resists being reduced to a checklist or process
- The methodology is appropriately defended by appeal to its philosophical depth, not just its operational results

These emphases would be different if the methodology were named with techne. Techne would emphasize craft mastery, skilled production, and the quality of the artifact. The implicit framing matters.

## Consequences

**What becomes easier:**

- The methodology's philosophical seriousness is signaled by the name
- Connecting to the Aristotelian and post-Aristotelian tradition is natural
- The emphasis on practitioner formation is justified by the name

**What becomes harder:**

- Readers unfamiliar with the philosophical tradition need orientation
- The name does not signal the artifact-producing dimension as clearly as techne would
- Some readers may find "praxis" pretentious; the methodology has to earn the name's weight
