# Monetization

This document treats the question of how Aim Praxis becomes economically self-sustaining. It is a candid analysis of paths and their realistic returns, not a marketing strategy.

The methodology itself is not for sale. Its development is funded by other work. The question is what economic relationships could grow from the methodology over time.

## The strategic frame

Two considerations shape the monetization analysis:

**Ship Audit first.** Mark Schilling's primary income-replacement vehicle is Ship Audit, the SaaS product whose development is the methodology's first significant application. Methodology work that distracts from Ship Audit's success is mis-prioritized. Methodology work that supports Ship Audit's success is doubly valuable.

**The methodology is the moat.** The compounding artifacts the methodology produces are increasingly the source of Ship Audit's defensibility. Methodology development is therefore not separate from product development; it is the long-term investment that makes the product defensible.

This frame reorders the apparent monetization paths.

## Monetization paths, ranked by recommended sequence

### Path 1: Ship Audit as the methodology's case study

The strongest near-term move is to use Ship Audit as the worked example for Aim Praxis. The methodology's credibility is proven by a working product built using it. Ship Audit's success is the methodology's first proof.

Revenue: indirect, through Ship Audit's subscription revenue.

Time horizon: 12-24 months to meaningful revenue from Ship Audit.

Effort: this is the priority work regardless of methodology.

### Path 2: Substack documentation

Building public documentation of the methodology on Substack costs nothing and compounds slowly. Each post is a small investment with low risk and discoverable returns.

Revenue: paid subscriptions at $5-15/month, building over time. Realistic ceiling: a few hundred paid subscribers at $5/month produces a few thousand dollars per month.

Time horizon: months to first revenue, years to meaningful revenue.

Effort: weekly or bi-weekly writing, drawing on the methodology development that's happening anyway.

The Substack does double duty as marketing for Ship Audit, building Mark's profile in the SoPK and software methodology spaces.

### Path 3: Book

A book consolidates the methodology into a referenceable form. Books in this category (methodology, business, technical) typically produce modest direct revenue but significant credibility leverage.

Revenue: book royalties typically modest ($10K-50K range for a successful but not bestselling book). Indirect revenue through consulting and speaking can be much larger.

Time horizon: 18-36 months from start to publication.

Effort: significant — book-writing is a multi-quarter project.

Strategic timing: after Ship Audit has a working revenue base, before consulting or platform work is prioritized.

### Path 4: Consulting

Aim Praxis as a consulting engagement framework. Engagements would typically run $50K-$200K and last 3-12 months.

Revenue: high per-engagement, but constrained by Mark's time. Realistic ceiling: 2-4 engagements per year at $100K each = $200K-400K annually.

Time horizon: requires book or substantial public documentation as credentialing; 18-30 months from start to first engagement.

Effort: each engagement is significant client work.

Trade-off: consulting time is time not spent on Ship Audit and not spent on methodology development. The trade-off has to be considered explicitly.

### Path 5: Workshops and training

Multi-day workshops at organizations adopting the methodology. Typical pricing $5K-$15K per participant for multi-day intensive training.

Revenue: per-workshop revenue is high but throughput is constrained by Mark's time.

Time horizon: similar to consulting; requires credentialing through documentation or book.

Effort: workshop preparation is one-time; delivery is intensive but bounded.

### Path 6: Agentic toolkit as products

ATDD Guardian is already published. Other agents in the methodology (refinement, workshopping, glossary, PDSA) could be published similarly as MCP servers or as Claude Code skills.

Revenue: open-source publication produces credentialing but not direct revenue. Paid versions or enterprise support could produce revenue.

Time horizon: each agent is a project; full toolkit is 12-24 months.

Strategic value: positions Mark as a builder of the agentic substrate, not just a writer about it.

### Path 7: Certification

Long-term, a certification program could produce per-certification revenue. The Scrum Alliance pattern is the obvious model.

Revenue: per-certification revenue depends on adoption; full programs take years to scale.

Time horizon: 3-5+ years.

Effort: significant ongoing infrastructure.

Strategic risk: certification programs often become detached from the methodology's substance, producing credentials without practitioners. Worth doing only if the substance is preserved.

### Path 8: Platform/SaaS

A platform that implements the methodology — story-map management with refinement agent, glossary management with glossary agent, etc. — could become a product.

Revenue: subscription SaaS revenue model, comparable to Ship Audit.

Time horizon: 24-48 months from concept to revenue.

Strategic note: this is Ship Audit's pattern applied to the methodology itself. It would be a significant pivot if pursued; not appropriate while Ship Audit is the primary vehicle.

## Recommended sequence

1. Ship Audit first. The product whose development is the methodology's first proof.
2. Substack second. Low-cost, low-risk documentation that compounds.
3. Book third. After Ship Audit has revenue and the Substack has audience.
4. Consulting fourth. After the book provides credentialing.
5. Toolkit publication ongoing throughout. Each agent published produces a marker of progress.
6. Workshops if asked. Not pursued actively while consulting is the time-constrained channel.
7. Certification deferred. Not appropriate until the methodology has hundreds of practitioners.
8. Platform deferred. Not appropriate while Ship Audit is the primary build.

## What this implies

The methodology's economic value is realized over a decade, not a quarter. Each path takes longer than it seems. The compounding artifacts of the methodology compound the economic returns the same way they compound the technical returns.

Patience is the strategic asset. The temptation to monetize prematurely produces worse outcomes than the discipline of letting the methodology mature alongside Ship Audit.
