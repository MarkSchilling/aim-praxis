# Intellectual Lineage

Aim Praxis stands on multiple traditions. This document names them, explains what each contributes, and notes where the contribution appears in the methodology.

## W. Edwards Deming and the System of Profound Knowledge

**Sources:** *Out of the Crisis* (1982), *The New Economics* (1993)

Deming's framework is the foundational lineage. His four lenses — appreciation for a system, knowledge of variation, theory of knowledge, psychology — supply the analytical vocabulary that organizes everything else.

What Deming contributes:

- The conviction that 94% of failures belong to the system rather than the individual
- The distinction between common-cause and special-cause variation
- The PDSA cycle as the unit of organizational learning
- Operational definitions as the foundation for testable theory
- The aim of a system as its central organizing concept
- The injunction against optimizing parts at the expense of the whole

Where it appears: throughout. The aim concept defines the system the methodology serves. The PDSA Improvement Agent operationalizes the PDSA cycle. Operational definitions structure every concept document. The rework register classifies variation and supports the common-cause/special-cause distinction.

## Systems Thinking

**Sources:** Jay Forrester (*Industrial Dynamics*, 1961), Donella Meadows (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008, posthumous), Peter Senge (*The Fifth Discipline*, 1990)

Systems thinking extends Deming's "appreciation for a system" into a fully developed analytical discipline with its own vocabulary and tools. The stock-and-flow modeling discipline, the typology of feedback loops, the catalog of system archetypes, and the boundary analysis all come from this tradition.

What systems thinking contributes:

- Stocks and flows as the primary unit of analysis
- Reinforcing and balancing feedback loops as named, separable structures
- Cycle time as a first-class property of any loop
- System archetypes (pack rat, limits to growth, fixes that fail, shifting the burden)
- Boundary as a deliberate analytical choice
- The distinction between events, patterns, and structures as levels of understanding

Where it appears: the stock-and-flow analysis of the story map, the BMC, and the VPC. The five-loop architecture organized by cycle time. The named archetypes (pack rat, inner-loop dominance). The boundary analysis of inflow sources and outflow destinations.

## Lean and the Toyota Production System

**Sources:** Taiichi Ohno (*Toyota Production System*, 1988), James Womack and Daniel Jones (*Lean Thinking*, 1996)

The lean tradition shares Deming's roots and develops complementary insights about production systems specifically. The emphasis on flow, the elimination of waste, the discipline of stopping the line, and the just-in-time principle all inform Aim Praxis.

What lean contributes:

- Pull over push as a coordination principle
- Stopping the line when defects are detected, rather than passing them downstream
- The distinction between value-adding work and waste
- Kaizen as continuous improvement at every level
- The respect for workers' expertise as essential to system improvement

Where it appears: the gate-enforcement pattern (stopping the line when criteria are not met). The treatment of scope depletion as a legitimate outflow rather than a failure. The recognition that not all work in the system is value-adding.

## Story Mapping

**Source:** Jeff Patton, *User Story Mapping* (2014)

Patton's contribution is the recognition that user stories alone lose the narrative coherence that makes a product comprehensible. The story map preserves the journey while still permitting the granularity that agile delivery requires.

What story mapping contributes:

- The backbone-and-ribs structure
- The narrative flow as a primary organizing dimension
- The release slicing technique as a way to ship coherent products incrementally
- The opening-game/mid-game/end-game framing of development strategy
- The recognition that "the map is not the territory" — the conversation matters more than the artifact

Where it appears: directly as the Story Mapping practice. The aging-chain analysis of the story map extends Patton's framework with explicit stock-and-flow vocabulary.

## Behavior-Driven Development

**Sources:** Dan North (early articles, ~2003-2006), Liz Keogh, Chris Matts, Gojko Adzic (*Specification by Example*, 2011, and *Bridging the Communication Gap*, 2009)

BDD's contribution is the discipline of expressing behavior in a vocabulary shared between business and engineering, and making that vocabulary executable. The Given-When-Then structure and the emphasis on ubiquitous language come from this tradition.

What BDD contributes:

- The Given-When-Then scenario structure
- Ubiquitous language as a first-class artifact
- Living documentation as the emergent output of executable specification
- The conversation-first orientation — examples come from conversation, not from imagination
- The bridge between domain-driven design and test-driven development

Where it appears: directly as the BDD practice. The ubiquitous language glossary is treated as a compounding artifact maintained by the glossary agent. The workshopping agent operationalizes the BDD authoring discipline.

## Acceptance Test-Driven Development

**Sources:** Lasse Koskela (*Test Driven*, 2007), the FIT tradition (Ward Cunningham and others), Gojko Adzic's contributions

ATDD predates BDD historically and emphasizes customer-engineering collaboration at the story level. The discipline of writing acceptance tests before code, with customer involvement in the test definition, is the central contribution.

What ATDD contributes:

- The "three amigos" pattern of product, engineering, and testing collaborating on acceptance
- Executable acceptance criteria as the bridge between user intent and engineering output
- The customer's voice in defining "done" at the story level
- The use of acceptance tests as both specification and verification

Where it appears: directly as the ATDD practice. ATDD Guardian operationalizes the deterministic gate-checking of acceptance criteria. The boundary between BDD (behavior specification) and ATDD (story acceptance) is preserved with distinct documents and distinct gates.

## Test-Driven Development

**Source:** Kent Beck, *Test-Driven Development: By Example* (2002)

TDD's contribution is the discipline of writing tests before code, in a tight loop, as a design technique rather than a verification technique. The red-green-refactor cycle is the canonical TDD rhythm.

What TDD contributes:

- The red-green-refactor cycle as the unit-level inner loop
- Tests as design pressure that improves code structure
- The discipline of taking small steps to maintain a working system at all times
- The recognition that tests serve multiple purposes (specification, verification, regression protection, design feedback)

Where it appears: directly as the TDD practice. The placement of the coding agent inside the work-in-progress stock respects TDD's discipline rather than replacing it.

## Continuous Delivery

**Source:** Jez Humble and David Farley, *Continuous Delivery* (2010)

The continuous delivery movement extends agile's "potentially shippable increment" to the operational reality of actually shipping continuously. The discipline of keeping the main branch always deployable, the investment in deployment automation, and the recognition of deployment as a first-class concern come from this tradition.

What continuous delivery contributes:

- The discipline of keeping the system always deployable
- Deployment pipelines as a system component
- The recognition that long release cycles hide problems and discourage learning
- The integration of operational concerns into the development cycle

Where it appears: directly as the CI/CD practice. The observability agent extends the CD pattern with production monitoring that closes the learning loop back to VPC.

## Philosophical Tradition

**Sources:** Aristotle (*Nicomachean Ethics*, particularly Books VI and X), Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologica*, particularly the treatments of habitus and prudentia), Dallas Willard (*The Spirit of the Disciplines*, 1988)

The methodology's name and several of its central concepts come from a philosophical and theological tradition that distinguishes praxis from techne, names phronesis (practical wisdom) as the virtue of acting well in specific situations, and treats disciplined practice as both intellectually serious and spiritually formative.

What this tradition contributes:

- The concept of praxis itself — practice informed by theory, action emerging from understanding
- Phronesis as the irreducibly human capacity for judgment in specific situations
- Habitus as the disposition that disciplined practice forms over time
- The dignity of skilled work as a form of moral and intellectual engagement
- The recognition that good practice requires good practitioners, not just good rules

Where it appears: the methodology's name. The human judgment zone explicitly preserves phronesis. The treatment of compounding artifacts reflects the formation of habitus through disciplined practice over time. The respect for human practitioners distinguishes Aim Praxis from methodologies that treat humans as interchangeable executors.

## What Aim Praxis adds

Aim Praxis is not merely a synthesis of these traditions. It contributes something specific: the explicit integration of Deming's quality discipline with broader systems thinking, applied to the seven-practice software development stack, with deliberate placement of agentic augmentation at points where it widens valves without replacing human judgment.

This integration is what no prior methodology has done. Agile methodologies use systems thinking implicitly. Lean methodologies use Deming explicitly but stop short of the multi-time-scale agile practices. Specification by Example and Domain-Driven Design develop the BDD/ubiquitous-language thread but do not integrate the broader system. Continuous Delivery operationalizes the inner loop without the outer-loop discipline that ensures the right things get shipped.

Aim Praxis is the methodology that holds all seven cycle times in view simultaneously, grounds the discipline in two complementary intellectual traditions (Deming and systems thinking), and explicitly designs for the era when AI agents can do consistent mechanical work but cannot exercise judgment about purpose.

## What this lineage implies

The methodology's defensibility against the charge "this is just another agile methodology" rests on this lineage. Aim Praxis is the only methodology I know of that names Deming, systems thinking, and the seven specific practices as co-equal contributors, and that places agents within an explicit human-judgment framework rather than treating agents as either threats or panaceas.

The right way to introduce Aim Praxis is as the continuation of a forty-year tradition, not as a new invention. It is what Deming would have built if he had lived to see continuous delivery and agentic AI. It is what systems-thinking practitioners would have built if they had committed to a specific software methodology rather than to a discipline of analysis. It is what the BDD and ATDD pioneers would have built if they had integrated their work with the broader system around it.

The lineage is the credibility. Use it.
